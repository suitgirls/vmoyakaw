The Young Woman Who Broke Japan's Secret Code: Genevieve Grotjan's Hidden Heroism

Valentina Oyakawa / July 22th, 2025

Genevieve Grotjan was a brilliant American mathematician and cryptanalyst who played a key role during World War II. She worked at the Signal Intelligence Service (SIS), which was part of the United States Army. Her main job was to analyze and break enemy codes, and her most significant accomplishment was discovering the crucial pattern that led to breaking the Japanese diplomatic cipher machine known as PURPLE. This machine encoded highly secret messages between Japanese diplomats, and breaking it allowed the U.S. to read Japan's ommunications before and during World War II. In September 1940, Genevieve, at just 27 years old, identified the repeating patterns in the PURPLE code that no one else had seen before. Her discovery made it possible for American cryptanalysts to build a machine that could decipher Japanese messages in real time. This breakthrough gave the United States valuable information about Japan's diplomatic and military plans. She worked at Arlington Hall in Virginia, which became a center for code-breaking efforts during the war. Her work took place mainly between 1939 and 1945, but her PURPLE breakthrough was in 1940. Genevieve Grotjan's intelligence, determination, and attention to detail changed the course of wartime code breaking. Although she worked behind the scenes and did not seek fame, her contributions saved countless lives and strengthened national security. Today, she is remembered as one of the pioneering women who proved that cryptanalysis was not only for men but for any brilliant and dedicated mind.
